<Section icon={Target} title="Step-by-Step Guide: Squeezing Through the Corkscrew">

The Corkscrew and the Birthing Canal represent the first major physical bottlenecks in the cave. This section provides a step-by-step breakdown of how to navigate these tight spaces without panic or physical injury. The limestone walls in this area exert constant physical pressure on the body, making proper positioning essential.

Before entering the canal, ensure all loose gear is secured. The passage twists sharply, and any protruding items can cause you to become wedged against the rock face.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Enter the Birthing Canal">
    Position your body stomach-up and keep your arms extended straight ahead. Keeping your arms aligned with your torso minimizes shoulder width and prevents your shoulders from locking against the narrow walls.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Navigate the First Bend">
    Shimmy forward slowly using your feet and calves for leverage. Breathe steadily and deeply; exhaling fully allows your chest cavity to compress slightly, providing valuable extra inches of clearance in the tightest segments.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Execute the Corkscrew Twist">
    As the passage bends, rotate your torso to the right, then execute a hard left turn to match the natural spiral of the limestone. Do not force your way through; if you feel stuck, slide back slightly and adjust your angle.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Exit into the Main Hall">
    Slide feet-first into the opening of the Main Hall. Allow team members already in the chamber to guide your feet onto the landing ledge, ensuring a secure transition out of the horizontal squeeze.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="info" title="Psychological Management">
Claustrophobia is a common challenge in the Birthing Canal. If you feel panic rising, close your eyes, focus entirely on slow, rhythmic breathing, and remember that your team is positioned on both sides to assist you.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Swords} title="Conquering the Vertical Drop to the Main Hall">

Once past the horizontal crawls, the cave terrain changes dramatically. The path drops straight down, requiring cavers to transition from crawling to vertical climbing. The Main Hall is the second-largest chamber in the cave, but it serves as a gateway to a sheer vertical drop that must be negotiated to reach the final floor.

While professional cavers utilize advanced climbing harnesses and mechanical descenders, the local community has established a system of knotted ropes along the descent path. However, free climbing this section carries extreme risks and requires exceptional upper-body strength.

| Phase | Depth Transition | Primary Technique | Safety Margin |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Upper Drop | 50 to 70 Feet | Hand-over-hand rope descent | Moderate (use knotted rope loops) |
| Mid Ledge | 70 to 85 Feet | Foot placement search | Low (narrow standing ledge) |
| Final Drop | 85 to 100+ Feet | Upper body control, lower assist | Critical (requires ground spotter) |

The final drop is the most intense portion of the entire climb. The rock faces are often damp, reducing friction and making foot placement highly unstable. Navigators must rely on upper-body strength to control their descent speed while searchingly feeling for foot holds along the wall.

<Callout type="warning" title="Rope Integrity Warning">
Pre-placed ropes inside the cave are exposed to constant moisture and mud, which can degrade the fibers over time. Always test the weight capacity of any pre-placed rope before committing your full body weight to the descent.
</Callout>
